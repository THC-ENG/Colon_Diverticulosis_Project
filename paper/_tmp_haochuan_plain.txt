II. DATASET
A. Data Sources and Low-Annotation Protocol
In this study, we construct a low-annotation-budget polyp
segmentation benchmark using multiple public colonoscopy
datasets. The training universe is built from Kvasir-SEG, CVCClinicDB, and selected training centers of PolypGen. KvasirSEG provides 1000 polyp images with pixel-wise annotations,
CVC-ClinicDB contains 612 annotated images, and PolypGen offers a large-scale multi-center resource with 1537 still
images, 2225 positive video sequences, and 4275 negative
frames.To further improve cross-center diversity,we use still
images together with sparsely sampled positive video frames
from the PolypGen. By combining these datasets, we aim to
cover broader variations in lesion appearance, imaging conditions, and center-specific domain shifts, thereby providing
a more realistic setting for evaluating label-efficient medical
image segmentation.
Although several source datasets provide full pixel-wise
annotations, we intentionally expose only a small subset of
labeled samples during training in order to simulate the
practical scenario of limited expert supervision. Specifically,
the training universe is partitioned into a small labeled set
Lsmall and a large unlabeled set Ularge , where Lsmall is used
for teacher adaptation and supervised student learning, while
Ularge is used for pseudo-label generation and semi-supervised

For samples in Lsmall , the ground-truth masks are taken
from the officially released annotations or from expert-verified
masks, depending on the source dataset and the final experimental setting. These annotations serve as the only humansupervised pixel-level references used to adapt the teacher
model and anchor the student model to clinically reliable
boundaries. In this way, the proposed framework does not
assume abundant manual annotation, but instead treats a
limited number of high-quality masks as the core supervision
resource.
For the unlabeled set Ularge , pseudo labels are generated
by the domain-adapted MedSAM teacher. To improve pseudolabel reliability, a quality-control pipeline is introduced before
these masks are used for student training. Specifically, lowquality predictions are filtered based on confidence, connectedcomponent consistency, boundary smoothness, and regionsize plausibility. High-confidence pseudo labels are directly
included in the expanded training pool, medium-confidence
samples are optionally corrected through rapid manual inspection, and low-confidence cases are discarded or reserved as
hard samples for later refinement. This design forms a data
flywheel in which a small amount of expert knowledge is
progressively amplified into scalable supervision.
C. External Test Sets and Generalization Evaluation

To rigorously evaluate out-of-distribution performance, external testing is conducted on ETIS-LaribPolypDB, CVCColonDB, CVC-300, and held-out centers from PolypGen (C1,
C2, C3, C4). ETIS-LaribPolypDB is used as a challenging
external benchmark due to its difficult lesion appearances
and substantial distribution gap from common training sets.
CVC-ColonDB and CVC-300 serve as classical cross-dataset
evaluation sets, while the held-out PolypGen centers C5 and
C6 are used to assess cross-center generalization under multisource domain shift. None of these external sets participate in
model selection or hyperparameter tuning.
This evaluation protocol is designed to measure not only
in-domain segmentation accuracy but also the robustness of
the proposed framework under realistic deployment conditions. Since medical endoscopy systems often face differences
in equipment, operators, centers, and imaging styles, strong

III. METHODOLOGY
A. Problem Definition

To avoid information leakage, all samples are split at
the patient level or sequence level whenever such metadata
are available. Images or frames from the same patient, the
same examination, or the same video sequence are assigned
exclusively to a single subset. Within the training universe,
Lsmall and Ularge are constructed after this leakage-aware
partitioning, ensuring that the teacher adaptation, pseudo-label
generation, and student training are all performed under a
consistent and reproducible protocol.
In practice, the exact proportion of Lsmall can be varied to
simulate different annotation budgets, such as ultra-low-label,
low-label, and moderate-label settings. This enables systematic
analysis of how the proposed teacher-student framework behaves as the amount of real supervision changes, and whether
the performance gain mainly comes from teacher adaptation,
pseudo-label expansion, or student-side architectural improvements.

B. Overall Teacher-Student Framework
The overall framework follows a teacher-student paradigm
with a closed-loop data expansion strategy. First, a MedSAMbased teacher is adapted to the polyp domain using a small
number of expert-annotated samples. Second, the adapted
teacher is used to generate pseudo labels for the large
unlabeled pool. Third, a frequency-decoupled ResSwinUNet
student is trained on both real labels and high-quality pseudo
labels, optionally under additional distillation supervision from
the teacher. In this way, the framework forms a complete
pipeline from scarce annotation to scalable training and finally
to efficient deployment.
The proposed framework decouples the roles of foundationmodel supervision and task-specific deployment. The teacher
model is used to absorb scarce expert knowledge and convert
it into scalable pseudo supervision and soft structural priors,
whereas the student model is designed to serve as the final
inference engine. This design addresses the practical contradiction that MedSAM provides strong medical segmentation
priors but is not ideal for real-time deployment due to its
interaction cost and computational overhead
C. SAM-LoRA Teacher Adaptation and Pseudo-Label Generation
The teacher model is initialized from MedSAM and adapted
to the target polyp domain using parameter-efficient low-rank
adaptation (LoRA). Instead of updating all parameters, LoRA
injects trainable low-rank matrices into selected attentionrelated layers, allowing the model to learn domain-specific
appearance priors with substantially reduced optimization cost.
In this study, the adapted teacher is expected to better capture
polyp-specific color distribution, mucosal texture, shape variation, and blurry lesion boundaries than the original generic
model.
During pseudo-label generation, box prompts, point
prompts, or candidate regions from a coarse localization stage
are used to trigger mask generation on unlabeled images. The
teacher is therefore not treated merely as an annotation tool,
but as a prior-rich supervisory model whose output can be
filtered, refined, and subsequently transferred to the student.
This role transformation is essential for converting scarce
expert masks into scalable supervision.
D. Two-Round SAM-LoRA Data Flywheel
To expand supervision beyond Lsmall , we construct a tworound SAM-LoRA data flywheel. In Round 0, MedSAM is
adapted on Lsmall to obtain the initial teacher T0 . In Round 1,
T0 is applied to the entire unlabeled pool Ularge , and the resulting masks are filtered according to confidence, boundary quality, connected-component consistency, and region plausibility.
The top 30%–40% high-quality pseudo-labeled samples are
then added into the training pool. In Round 2, the remaining
difficult unlabeled samples are reprocessed using the improved

3

teacher or the intermediate student-assisted system, and an
additional 10%–20% of reliable pseudo-labeled samples are
incorporated. After the second round, the flywheel stops rather
than expanding indefinitely.
In practical terms, the two-round design reflects a deliberate
trade-off between supervision gain and noise accumulation.
High-confidence pseudo labels are directly accepted, mediumconfidence masks may undergo fast manual correction, and
low-confidence predictions are moved into a hard-sample pool.
The expansion process is terminated once the gain becomes
saturated, when the external validation Dice improves by
less than a predefined threshold, or when the manual spotcheck pass rate of newly added pseudo labels falls below an
acceptable level.
E. Frequency-Decoupled ResSwinUNet Student Network
As shown in Fig. 2, the proposed student network adopts
a hybrid CNN-Transformer encoder-decoder architecture,
termed ResSwinUNet, to balance local representation, global
contextual modeling, and deployment efficiency. The overall
framework consists of a shallow residual convolutional encoder, two Swin-based semantic stages, a wavelet bottleneck,
and a U-shaped decoder with attention-gated skip connections.
Instead of simply stacking standard modules, the design is
tailored to polyp segmentation, where subtle lesion boundaries,
mucosal texture interference, and cross-domain appearance
variation must be handled simultaneously.
The encoder follows a progressive hybrid design. As illustrated in Fig. 2(a) and Fig. 2(b), given an input image I ∈
RH×W ×3 , the shallow ResNet branch first extracts multi-scale
convolutional features {x0, x1, x2}, which is responsible for
extracting stable low-level visual patterns, including mucosal
texture, specular reflections, and coarse lesion boundaries. And
the subsequent Swin stages strengthen long-range semantic
interaction at deeper levels, each Swin stage begins with
a strided convolutional downsampling layer and is followed
by a stack of Swin blocks to progressively refine semantic
representations.This combination is motivated by the fact that
polyp regions are often locally ambiguous but globally distinguishable only in relation to surrounding mucosal structures.
Therefore, the hybrid encoder allows the network to retain
texture sensitivity in early stages while acquiring stronger
semantic discrimination in later stages.
To alleviate the loss of boundary-sensitive details at the
deepest stage, the bottleneck feature x4 is further processed
by a wavelet bottleneck, as shown in Fig. 2(e). A Haar
discrete wavelet transform (DWT) is first applied to decompose x4 into one low-frequency component LL and three
high-frequency components LH, HL and HH. The lowfrequency branch preserves coarse semantic structure and is
compressed by a lightweight 1 × 1 projection, while the
concatenated high-frequency branch is sent to a dedicated
HighF requencyRef iner. The refined frequency components
are then fused through an inverse discrete wavelet transform
(IDWT), followed by a 3 × 3 convolutional fusion layer. This
design explicitly compensates for the common tendency of
deep encoder-decoder networks to lose high-frequency lesion
details during semantic compression.

Starting from the refined bottleneck representation, the
decoder produces hierarchical features {d4 , d3 , d2 , d1 }, where
intermediate decoder outputs may optionally be equipped with
auxiliary supervision heads for training stabilization. The final
full-resolution feature is mapped to the segmentation logits by
a lightweight prediction head. In our final framework, contour
sensitivity is primarily attributed to the wavelet- bottleneck
and the logits-edge consistency supervision, while auxiliary
heads, if used, are treated as optional components rather than
the core contribution.

F. Edge Consistency and Distillation Learning

Instead of relying solely on a heavy explicit boundary
branch, we introduce a logits-edge consistency constraint to
regularize the main segmentation output. Specifically, edge
responses are extracted from the predicted segmentation logits
using a gradient operator such as Sobel filtering, and are then
matched against boundary maps derived from the corresponding masks. This strategy enables the network to learn contouraware representations directly from the main segmentation
branch, thereby enhancing boundary sharpness with limited
structural overhead.
In addition to pseudo-label supervision, a teacher-student
distillation scheme is adopted to transfer soft structural priors
from the adapted teacher to the student. The first component
is response distillation, which encourages the student’s probability map to align with the teacher’s soft mask output rather
than only its binarized pseudo label. The second component
is edge distillation, which transfers contour-related knowledge
from the teacher to the student. Together, these two forms
of distillation help the student learn not only hard region
assignments but also smoother uncertainty-aware structural
cues.

To mitigate optimization conflict among these objectives,
we adopt a joint strategy composed of structural decoupling, spatial task allocation, stage-wise training, and dynamic loss weighting. Structural decoupling is achieved by
the wavelet-based separation of low-frequency semantics and
high-frequency boundaries, reducing direct competition within
a single shared representation. Spatial task allocation assigns
different supervision signals to different regions, where highconfidence regions receive harder pseudo-label supervision,
uncertain boundary bands receive softer distillation, and narrow ground-truth boundary regions are emphasized by the
edge-consistency term. Stage-wise training progressively introduces losses, typically starting from real-label segmentation,
then adding edge consistency, followed by pseudo-label supervision, and finally distillation. Dynamic weighting further
ensures that edge loss starts with a small coefficient, pseudolabel loss is gradually warmed up, and distillation is introduced
later with a relatively conservative weight.

region prediction, sharper contour modeling, and teacherguided structural priors without suffering from severe instability caused by naively optimizing all losses with equal
